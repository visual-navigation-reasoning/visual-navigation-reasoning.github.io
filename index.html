Reasoning in Visual Navigation of End-to-end Trained Agents:

A Dynamical Systems Approach

Anonymous authors

PDF (coming soon)


Abstract

Progress in embodied AI has led to end-to-end trained agents navigating in real photo-realistic environments capable of high-level reasoning and zero-shot or language conditioned behavior, with evaluations and benchmarks dominated by simulation. In this work, we focus on the behavior of fast-moving real robots and fine-grained behavior and present a large-scale experimental study involving 142 navigation episodes in a real environment and done with a real physical robot, analyzing the type of reasoning emerging from end-to-end training. In particular, we study the presence of realistic dynamics learned by the agent and used for open-loop forecasting, and its interplay with sensing. We analyze the way the agent uses latent memory and show that it holds elements of the scene structure and exploration information. We probe the agent's planning capabilities, and while we did not find evidence in precise motion plans in the agent's memory, in post-hoc analysis we show that its learned value function is linked to long-term plans. Put together, the experiments paint a new picture on how using tools from computer vision and sequential decision taking have led to new capabilities in robotics and control.

We simulate the robot's behavior using a second order dynamical model: \[ \begin{array}{ll} \ddot v(t) &= \frac{1}{\tau} \big(v(t) - c_v(t)\big) + \frac{2\gamma}{\tau} \dot v(t) \\ \ddot \omega(t) &= \frac{1}{\tau} \big(\omega(t) - c_\omega(t)\big) + \frac{2\gamma}{\tau} \dot \omega(t) \end{array} \] where

  • \(v(t)\) and \(\omega(t)\) are the robot’s linear and angular velocities at time \(t\),
  • \(\tau\) and \(\gamma\) represent the damping and response time of the model, and
  • \(c_v(t)\) and \(c_\omega(t)\) are the commands for linear and angular velocities.

These parameters are based on real data collected from the robot. Different damping and response time values are applied depending on the action type (acceleration or braking) and motion type (linear or angular).

Prediction vs. Correction

To navigate effectively, an agent can either rely solely on sensory inputs or use a learned dynamics model, integrating each step with feedback from prior actions. It’s believed that trained navigation agents combine both approaches through a prediction-correction process similar to a Kalman filter, where predictions from a dynamics model are adjusted with sensory feedback.

Dynamics agent uses a prediction-correction scheme to estimate its current pose.

Prediction vs. Correction

We measure the robustness of the agent against variation in dynamics/odometry parameters. Its sensitivity informs us about the agent's reliance on prediction and correction.

Click here for more details

To assess the trained policy's sensitivity to changes in inputs and dynamics, we test it in corrupted environments and measure the performance drop. We disturb the dynamics by altering the damping factor, response time, or max velocity, and corrupt inputs by adding noise to odometry sensors.

Since environment parameters vary in scale and meaning, we use a distance to belief metric to unify their effects on agent performance. This metric measures the average spatial difference between agent positions in the corrupted and training environments over 1,000 five-second action sequences.

How to use

The X axis represents the distance to belief as introduced above, and the Y axis corresponds to the selected metric. You can change the metric by clicking on the buttons above the plot.

On mouse hover, the graph open a tooltip showing a sampled trajectory generated with in-domain parameters (in black) and corrupted ones (in red).

Keep in mind that the red path is theoretical. In practice, the agent reacts its out-of-domain observations and can correct its trajectory.
